The idea

If an ordinary clock would tell time in the form

10  :  06  :  47.

then a “Chinese Remainder Clock” tells this same time in the form

1  2  :  0  2  1  :  1  3  2 .

These digits are the remainders after dividing:
  • hours by 3 and 4,
  • minutes by 3, 4, and 5, and
  • seconds by 3, 4, and 5.
A famous result called the Chinese Remainder Theorem promises that:
  • if you know the 3-remainder and the 4-remainder for the hour, then you can reconstruct the hour;
  • if you know the 3-remainder, the 4-remainder, and the 5-remainder for the minute, then you can reconstruct the minute; and
  • if you know the 3-remainder, the 4-remainder, and the 5-remainder for the second, then you can reconstruct the second.
The important thing is that the numbers that we use for division have no common factors. For example, knowing the 2-remainder and the 6-remainder would not suffice to reconstruct the hour.

Options

If you touch the main screen, a toggle will appear that will let you select automatic or manual operation of the clock. If you select the “Settings” (wrench) icon in the toolbar, several options will appear:
  • Design options
    • an option to choose the clock’s the artistic design;
    • customizable colors for the clock; and
    • how to order the units when drawing the clock;
  • Timekeeping options
    • whether to include the time as an ordinary digital clock;
    • whether to include the seconds; and
    • whether to show a 12-hour clock or a 24-hour clock.

Clock designs

We can classify the clock designs into two basic kinds: Circular and Polygonal.

Circular designs

A traditional, analog clock has hands that point to the correct time. For the hour, there are 12 positions on the circle, and for the minute, there are 60 positions. The hands rotate in “clockwise” fashion and you can read the time instantly by simply looking at the hands’ positions.

A circular Chinese Remainder Clock design behaves similarly. Using one circle for each remainder we consider:
  • the hour has one circle with three positions and one circle with four positions;
  • the minute has one circle with three positions, one circle with four positions, and one circle with five positions; and
  • the second has one circle with three positions, one circle with four positions, and one circle with five positions.
The position for the zero remainder lies at the top of each circle. Increasing a remainder by 1 means to rotate the position on the corresponding circle one step clockwise. The correct position on a circle is highlighted by either a ball or an arc of a circle. In Manual mode one can change the time by dragging the object along the circle.

Polygonal designs

Instead of considering positions on a circle, a digital clock simply lists the hours, minutes, and seconds.

A polygonal Chinese Remainder Clock design behaves similarly, but instead of just writing the remainders as numbers we represent them by modifying a triangle, square, pentagon, or octagon to convey whether we mean the 3-, 4-, 5-, or 8-remainder, respectively.
  • the hour has a triangle and a square (or, in the 24-hour clock, an octagon);
  • the minute has a triangle, a square, and a pentagon; and
  • the second has a triangle, a square, and a pentagon.
The polygonal designs included here exhibit a bit of variety in how they use this basic idea.

Automatic or Manual

When the “Auto/Manual” switch is set to “A”, the clock displays the phone’s current time. Pressing the toggle switches to “M” mode, where you can modify the time directly, and experiment with how the clock works. In this mode,
  • a “drop-down menu” lets you indicate whether you wish to modify the hour, minute, or second;
  • two buttons labeled “-1” and “+1” let you decrement or increment the selected unit of time; and
  • a text box lets you choose a value directly.
When in manual mode, some designs will let you use an intuitive touch-and-drag.

12- or 24-hour mode

Traditionally, time is measured in 12-hour increments, but many individuals and organizations tell time in 24-hour increments. The default for the Chinese Remainder Clock is to use 12-hour increments. In the settings, it is possible to choose 24-hour increments. Since 24 = 3 × 8, we now describe the hour using the 3-remainder and the 8-remainder (instead of the 4-remainder).
