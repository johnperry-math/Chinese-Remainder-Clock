The idea

If an ordinary clock would tell time in the form

10  :  06  :  47.

then a “Chinese Remainder Clock” tells this same time in the form

1  2  :  0  2  1  :  1  3  2 .

These digits are the remainders after dividing:

  • hours by 3 and 4,
  • minutes by 3, 4, and 5, and
  • seconds by 3, 4, and 5.
A famous result called the Chinese Remainder Theorem promises that:
  • if you know the 3-remainder and the 4-remainder for the hour, then you can reconstruct the hour;
  • if you know the 3-remainder, the 4-remainder, and the 5-remainder for the minute, then you can reconstruct the minute; and
  • if you know the 3-remainder, the 4-remainder, and the 5-remainder for the second, then you can reconstruct the second.
The important thing is that the numbers that we use for division have no common factors. For example, knowing the 2-remainder and the 6-remainder would not suffice to reconstruct the hour.

Options

Several toggles allow you to choose different modes:
  • a 12-hour clock or a 24-hour clock;
  • an analog clock or a digital clock;
  • a color clock or a black-and-white clock; and
  • automatic or manual.

12- or 24-hour mode

Traditionally, time is measured in 12-hour increments, but many individuals and organizations tell time in 24-hour increments. The default for the Chinese Remainder Clock is to use 12-hour increments, but since 24 = 3 × 8, we can divide the hours by 8 instead of by 4. Pressing the “12 hour” toggle will change from 12- to 24-hour mode; pressing that toggle again will change it back.

The “analog” Chinese Remainder Clock

A traditional clock has hands that point to the correct time. For the hour, there are 12 positions on the circle, and for the minute, there are 60 positions. We do the same thing here, using one circle for each remainder we consider:
  • the hour has one circle with three positions and one circle with four positions (the innermost rings of the dial);
  • the minute has one circle with three positions, one circle with four positions, and one circle with five positions (the outermost rings of the dial); and
  • the second has one circle with three positions, one circle with four positions, and one circle with five positions (interleaved among the outermost rings of the dial).
The position for the zero remainder lies at the top of each circle. Increasing a remainder by 1 means to rotate the position on the corresponding circle one step clockwise.

The “digital” Chinese Remainder Clock

Instead of considering positions on a circle, we can simply list the remainders. We write these numbers in a triangle, square, pentagon, or octagon to convey whether we mean the 3-, 4-, 5-, or 8-remainder.
  • the hour has a triangle and a square (or, in the 24-hour clock, an octagon), placed on the left;
  • the minute has a triangle, a square, and a pentagon, placed in the middle; and
  • the second has a triangle, a square, and a pentagon, placed on the right.

Automatic or Manual

When the “Auto” toggle is activated, the clock displays the phone’s current time. Pressing the toggle switches to a “Manual” mode, where you can modify the time directly, and experiment with how the clock works. In this mode,
  • a “drop-down menu” lets you indicate whether you wish to modify the hour, minute, or second;
  • two buttons labeled “-1” and “+1” let you decrement or increment the selected unit of time; and
  • a text box lets you choose a value directly.
